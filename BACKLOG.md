### docplan-has-no-business-day-calendar
- **`docplan` draws document dates without a business-day or holiday calendar, so documents land on weekends and public holidays at chance rates.** Found by the M11b board (document_plausibility) against `northgate-staffing` and verified directly from the manifest: **19 of 53 planned documents (36%) fall on a Saturday or Sunday**, against ~29% for a uniform draw. It is invisible for most genres, but minutes assert that a meeting happened: `d:0014` records a client working session at the client's offices on **Saturday 2016-05-28** (Memorial Day weekend) and `d:0050` records one on **Tuesday 2023-07-04**, US Independence Day, with the client's General Manager attending. Neither document remarks on it. The authors handle it well when they happen to notice (`d:0025` writes around its 2017-12-31 date: "Her arrival lands at the turn of the year, which is convenient rather than awkward"), which is luck, not a mechanism.
- **Why deferred:** out of scope for M11b, and it moves every date in every ledger, so it breaks the byte pin on all seven committed orgs and forces a full fleet regeneration -- the exact cost this turn just paid. It also needs a decision rather than a patch: a US-holiday table is era-dependent and locale-dependent, and the fleet already spans 1999-2025, so "which calendar" is a real question. A cheaper partial fix is to constrain only the genres that assert attendance (meeting_minutes, engagement_email).
- **Revisit criteria:** the fleet is regenerated for any other reason; or a recipe is written for a non-US locale, where the holiday question must be answered anyway; or a reader or board cites a weekend meeting as a tell (it has now been cited once).
- **Origin:** spec 2026-07-17 (M11b; found by the flagship board against northgate-staffing, confirmed by counting the manifest).

### concurrent-workers-share-one-scratchpad
- **Concurrently dispatched workers share one session scratchpad and can clobber each other's scratch files.** Affects both fan-out paths. Found first in MODEL-AB Round 2, where four board reviewers ran at once and one read another's extracted text, then asserted a checkable falsehood about the *other* arm (claiming byte-identical prose across arms; all 22 docs differed). Then observed on the live `/forge` path during the M11b fleet run: two concurrent forge-author workers overwrote each other's validation scripts, and one reported the other's batch failures under its own command. No bad artifact resulted either time -- the airlock accepted every batch, and per-arm review verdicts stayed sound because each reviewer read its own share -- but a worker that distrusts its own checks burns a turn, and a reviewer that reads a sibling's evidence can publish a false finding.
- **Why deferred:** `/forge` now tells each worker to namespace its scratch by work order (Step 3b), which held for the remaining four orgs of the fleet run, so the live path has a working mitigation. The real fix is harness-level per-worker scratch isolation, which OrgSmith does not control: the skill can only ask. `/forge-review` is per-slug over one org, so its six reviewers read the same share by design and have nothing to cross-contaminate; the reviewer half only bites a board dispatched across two or more orgs at once, which no committed skill does.
- **Scale note added 2026-07-17, and M12's spec must take a position on it.** The mitigation is prompt-level: the skill asks each worker to namespace its scratch, and a worker that ignores the ask fails silently. That held across 38 batches at M11b. The flagship is **~334 batches** (`docs/SCALE.md`), roughly nine times the exposure, and the failure is probabilistic rather than deterministic -- "held for four orgs" is not a rate. `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` makes the same point from outside and is right about it: "Prompting workers to namespace scratch files is a mitigation, not isolation." Neither observed instance produced a bad artifact (the airlock accepted every batch), so the honest framing is that this costs turns rather than correctness -- but that claim rests on the airlock catching what the scratchpad confuses, and it should be stated deliberately rather than assumed at nine times the batch count.
- **Revisit criteria:** the harness gains per-subagent scratch isolation (delete this then, and drop the prompt guidance from `/forge` Step 3b); or a board is dispatched over more than one org in a single session; or the prompt-level mitigation is observed failing -- a worker citing evidence that cannot be located in its own assigned batch; or M12 is specced, which multiplies the exposure by ~9 and is the first run large enough to turn a low per-batch probability into a likely event.
- **Origin:** spec 2026-07-17 (M11b; reviewer half found by MODEL-AB Round 2's harness, author half found live during the fleet run).

### cross-document-voice
- **Every genre collapses to one template across authors, years, and clients, and the instrument cannot see it.** The M11b board's largest cluster: 5 of 16 majors against `northgate-staffing`, all in `review/findings/cross_document_voice.json`. All six kickoff memos, by six authors over eight years, run "Workstreams" -> "Next Steps" -> a closing aphorism. Four of five engagement emails contain the literal string "Two asks. First... Second...". The board also reports the "I would rather X now than Y later" antithesis at **34 occurrences across 26 of 44 documents**, every author, every year 2015-2023.
- **That 34 is the board's judgment, not arithmetic, and no regex rescues it.** The figure is semantic: the board's cited evidence includes "This is easy to do from the start and impossible to retrofit" (`d:0011`), the rhetorical move without the construction. Measured over the 44 authored documents via `review/corpus.py`'s own prose extractor (2026-07-17), the count is entirely a function of what you decide to count: a strict temporal antithesis (`rather ... now/early ... than ... later/late`) finds **5 across 5 docs**; a looser `rather <word> ... than` finds **20 across 17**; the plain words `rather than` appear **146 times across 43 of 44 documents**, once per 200 words of a 29,138-word corpus. The board's 34 sits in the middle of a spectrum spanning 5 to 146, and nothing adjudicates. This entry published "20, the reproducible floor" for about an hour on 2026-07-17 before that was checked and found to be one arbitrary point dressed as a bound -- a smaller version of the same error the entry exists to describe.
- **Which is the finding underneath the finding.** The defect is real at every reading (146 occurrences across 43 of 44 documents is not a coincidence in any interpretation), but its *size* is unmeasurable, because no ledger owns "is this the same rhetorical figure". That is why the board is the only instrument that reports it and why the board cannot be trusted with the number. Quoted as opinion, per the README's rule that a finding which cannot be confirmed against a ledger is reported as opinion rather than fact. The README has said since M11b that this "is the genuinely hard one, and it has no scheduled fix" -- but it has never had an entry, which is how a known defect with no owner goes quiet.
- **The measurement gap is the important half.** `review/metrics.py` is *structurally* blind to it: northgate's own `GENERATION-REPORT.md` records "No same-genre pair reaches 0.15 4-gram Jaccard (highest: 0.0751)" against the corpus described above. `docs/REVIEW-CALIBRATION.md:81-87` predicted exactly this and named the mechanism: "documents a real firm would template *don't* repeat, while documents no template governs repeat *exactly* -- and both failures track authoring batch boundaries rather than author, client, or year. A same-genre similarity metric can only flag prose that repeats. It is structurally blind to prose that *fails* to repeat where house style requires it." Only the board finds this, and the board is the weakest instrument in the repo.
- **Why deferred:** no cheap fix is known. The cause is a fresh-context worker with no view of its siblings' prose, which is also what makes parallel authoring possible (M10) and what keeps the airlock honest. Showing each worker the corpus so far would serialize authoring and re-introduce the wall M10 exists to remove. Editing committed prose is forbidden by `/forge-review`'s own rule. The candidate fixes (a per-author style vector in the brief, a banned-construction list, a post-hoc voice pass) are all unproven and all cost either tokens or determinism.
- **Load-bearing for M12, which is why this is logged now rather than at the fix.** The failure tracks **batch boundaries**, and the flagship is ~334 batches against the 38 that built the whole fleet (`docs/SCALE.md`). Scaling the generator scales this defect along the exact axis that produces it, and no automated tier will report it. An external critique reached the same conclusion from outside (`docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md`: a large corpus from the current process "would amplify current artifacts rather than prove realism") without knowing the batch-boundary mechanism that makes it concrete. M12's spec must take an explicit position even if the position is "accepted, measured, published".
- **Revisit criteria:** M12 is specced (it must say something here); or a similarity instrument is proposed that can see structural rather than literal repetition; or any recipe brief gains per-author voice guidance for another reason; or a reader cites the tic without being pointed at it.
- **Origin:** spec 2026-07-17 (M11b board, five majors); logged 2026-07-17 after an external critique raised it independently and the sweep found no entry behind the README's claim that it is known.


### reporting-line-drift
- **Authored prose carries a reporting relationship the ledger owns, and disagrees with it.** `graph_acl_naturalness.json` major against `northgate-staffing`: "Both research associates hired after 2019 are told in their onboarding records that they report to the Managing Director, but the ledger reports them to the Principal, who is not named in either document." Quoted in the README's "What is not modeled today".
- **This is a hard-rule violation, not a realism gap, which is why it is filed separately from the board's other findings.** CLAUDE.md: "Facts are load-bearing; prose is replaceable. All numbers, dates, ids, names, and relationships come from the deterministic ledgers... Never let generated text carry a literal value that the ledger owns." A reporting line is a relationship the ledger owns. The placeholder mechanism exists exactly to make this impossible for fees and dates, and the org chart is simply not wired into it: `{{fact:...}}` covers planted facts, not `foundation.json`'s edges. The validator's org-chart rules check the *ledger* is acyclic and orphan-free; nothing checks the prose agrees with it.
- **Why deferred:** out of scope for M11b, which was a generation turn, and the fix is a design choice rather than a patch. Options: extend placeholders to graph edges (most correct, largest change, touches every brief); brief the author with the reporting line explicitly and lint the deliverable for contradicting role nouns (cheaper, weaker, a lint that must understand prose); or stop putting reporting lines in `firm_digest`'s brief at all (cheapest, loses real content). It also cannot be fixed in committed prose: the fleet is frozen and the rule against editing a fixture from board findings stands.
- **Revisit criteria:** M12 (the flagship's onboarding records will make the same claims, and at ~2,000 documents the drift is no longer anecdotal); or the placeholder mechanism is extended for any other reason; or a validator rule is proposed that reads prose against `foundation.json` edges; or a reader cites it as a correctness bug rather than a realism one, which is what it is.
- **Origin:** spec 2026-07-17 (M11b board, `graph_acl_naturalness`); logged 2026-07-17 -- the sweep found the README quoting it while no entry existed, and an external critique listed it among four "First:" priorities.

### generator-fingerprinting
- **A consumer can learn OrgSmith's tells rather than enterprise capability, and nothing here tests for it.** Raised by `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` and absent from this repo in any form. The candidate fingerprints are all real: a stable `Engagements/Finance/Firm/People` taxonomy, a filename grammar regular enough to parse with a regex (`YYYY.MM.DD - Genre - Client - vN FINAL.ext`), nine genre rows in one registry, one placeholder-to-surface rendering path, published location-hard-case policies, one OCR corruption profile, a uniform synthetic-provenance marker on every file, and prose from a single model family. A retrieval system that learns "status reports live two folders down and end in v2 FINAL" scores well here and has learned nothing about a real share.
- **The value of the entry is the reframe, not the news.** The evidence is already published: `cross-document-voice` records that every genre collapses to one template. We file that as a realism defect. It is *also* a benchmark-validity threat, and the second framing is the one that bites a consumer, because a fingerprint does not have to look unrealistic to be learnable. The same fact is a different problem depending on who is reading.
- **Why deferred:** the defenses are cheap individually and meaningless individually. Held-out recipes, hidden seeds, an unpublished flagship variant, adversarially altered directory and filename conventions, multiple authoring models, and (the critique's own preference) an independently implemented second generator only mean something against a stated threat model and a consumer who is actually scoring. Nobody is scoring yet. Building hold-outs before there is a claim to protect is scaffolding.
- **In tension with a published position, which is why this is a decision and not a task.** The README's whole strategy is that everything is browsable, checkable, and committed -- "nothing to clone, install, or authenticate". Held-out seeds and hidden recipes are the opposite of that by construction. M12 is scoped as a capability showpiece rather than a benchmark, so the tension does not bind yet; it binds the moment someone reports a score.
- **Revisit criteria:** any external party reports scores against a committed org (the entry becomes urgent, not merely open); or OrgSmith is submitted or cited as a benchmark rather than a generator; or a second authoring model is used for a committed org, which is the cheapest fingerprint defense and is already half-built (`docs/MODEL-AB.md`); or the flagship's recipe is considered for withholding.
- **Origin:** `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` (external critique, the one finding in it that was genuinely new).

### external-validity-program
- **Nothing establishes that a system scoring well on OrgSmith scores well on a real corpus, and the repo says so** (README, "What this does not prove"). `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` names the distinction precisely: **verification** (did the software implement its design correctly?) versus **validation** (is the design an adequate model of organizations?). OrgSmith is strong on the first -- 445 tests, a byte pin, 29 validator rules -- and has nothing on the second. The critique's proposed remedy is an "M11.5 validation milestone" producing a Flagship Validity Contract: declared use cases with claims and non-claims, measured target distributions (files per employee-year, emails per employee-day, thread-depth and response-time distributions, sender/recipient degree, document-type proportions, temporal burstiness, version-cluster sizes, graph motifs, missingness and error rates), a benchmark split strategy, a pre-registered transfer protocol, and downstream validation against at least one real corpus and ideally an independently constructed second generator.
- **Why deferred:** it is a research program, not a milestone, and it presumes a positioning decision that has been made the other way. **M12 is scoped as a capability showpiece, not a peer-reviewed benchmark** (user decision, 2026-07-17). Target distributions require a reference population OrgSmith does not have and cannot legally acquire for most of the listed statistics; transfer experiments require a real corpus, a scoring harness, and several systems to run; an independent second generator is a second project. Adopting the frame would also mean claiming a scientific standard the project would then have to hold, which is worse than not claiming it.
- **What was adopted instead, so this entry is not read as a rejection:** the parts that are cheap and stand alone. `emit-evals` already ships scored suites for four of the critique's seven declared use cases (retrieval, extraction, entity resolution with alias credit and per-ambiguity-class recall, ACL-aware visibility), derived deterministically from ledgers; M12 takes the nested-splits idea (core / +distractors / +noise / full, one eval suite across all four) so the flagship produces a retrieval degradation curve rather than a headline number; and the noise model gives the denominator the critique correctly identifies as where real retrieval difficulty comes from.
- **The honest position it does license, and the README already takes:** develop and diagnose on OrgSmith, evaluate transfer on a real corpus, report the gap. Without the third step a good OrgSmith score is an internal result. That is a caveat the README states rather than a program the repo runs.
- **Revisit criteria:** OrgSmith is submitted, cited, or adopted as a benchmark rather than a generator (the non-claims stop being sufficient the moment someone else makes the claim); or an external party reports scores and asks what they transfer to; or a real organizational corpus with usable aggregate statistics becomes available under terms that permit publishing a comparison; or a collaborator arrives who wants the validation half as their contribution.
- **Origin:** `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md`; scoped out the same day.


### event-simulation
- **Documents are generated from a plan, not emitted by a simulated organization.** `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md`: "A real organization is a process generating artifacts, not a collection of individually plausible artifacts... Do not generate more documents. Generate a simulated organization whose activities naturally emit documents." The process-level tells it lists are real and mostly already logged here: engagement activity disappears for years while hiring continues, meetings land on weekends, prose contradicts the ledger's reporting lines, every document is deliberate. A discrete-event model (hiring, projects, billing, meetings, approvals, travel, deadlines, disputes, leave, reporting changes) would emit documents as observations of events rather than as registry rows.
- **Why deferred:** it is a rewrite of `fabric` and `docplan`, the two stages every committed org's ledgers derive from, and it would break the byte pin on all seven. It is also further along than the critique knew. **M9 already moved one step in exactly this direction**: `docplan/registry.py` replaced a fixed `2E + 7` genre skeleton with driver-derived supply -- each genre names a DRIVER (`per_engagement`, `per_fiscal_year`, `firm_periodic`, `per_hire`) and a CADENCE, so "document count falls out of the firm's real activity -- its engagements, fiscal years, and hires". That is events-emit-documents for four coarse event classes. The gap is that the drivers are structural facts rather than a simulated timeline: nothing decides that *this* meeting happened on *this* day because *that* deadline moved.
- **The cheaper intermediate is what M12 actually takes.** Most of the critique's process complaints have a knob-shaped fix that does not need a simulator: a business-day calendar constrains the genres that assert attendance; deriving revenue from the engagement book closes the fee/revenue gap; a noise model supplies the abandoned and superseded content. Each lands additively and leaves the fleet pinned. A simulator is what you build when the knobs stop composing, and they have not yet.
- **Revisit criteria:** two or more process defects arrive that cannot be expressed as independent knobs (the signal that the knobs have stopped composing); or a recipe needs causal dependency between events (a slipped deadline moving a meeting moving a status report); or `fabric` is rewritten for any other reason; or a flagship-scale board finds process incoherence the knobs were supposed to fix.
- **Origin:** `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` ("Realism currently stops at the document boundary").


### packaging-and-archival
- **The package is not installable, and `pyproject.toml` says so as intent rather than oversight:** "Project metadata and pytest configuration only. The package is run from the repo root (python -m orgsmith); it is not built or installed." No `[build-system]`, no `[project.scripts]`, no `[project.dependencies]`, so `pip install .` fails outright. No container either, which matters more than it looks: `weasyprint` needs system Pango and `render/legacy.py` shells out to LibreOffice, and both are documented in comments rather than reproducible by any artifact in the repo (`doctor` detects their absence at runtime instead). `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` asks for an installable versioned package, locked dependencies, a container image, a machine-readable CLI spec, and archival releases with checksums and DOI-backed storage.
- **One part of that ask is already done and the critique missed it:** dependencies are pinned exactly, all 16 runtime and 1 dev, each with a rationale comment, and a short-tier test enforces it (`test_requirements_are_pinned`). The fair narrower point survives: exact pins are not a hash-locked file and transitive deps float.
- **Why deferred:** this is release work, not milestone work, and doing it before M12 buys nothing M12 needs -- the flagship is generated on the dev box by the same skills as the fleet. It is also cheapest to do once, at the point where there is a version worth archiving. DOI-backed archival in particular is a claim about permanence that should follow the v2.0 corpus being final, not precede it.
- **Revisit criteria:** v2.0 is cut as a release (do it then, all of it, in one turn); or someone outside this box needs to run the generator rather than read its output, which is the first time `pip install` matters; or CI needs LibreOffice, which is the first time the container stops being optional; or the corpus is cited somewhere that needs a stable identifier.
- **Origin:** `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` ("Packaging is too informal").


### state-json-mixes-execution-and-provenance
- **`state.json` is simultaneously mutable run state and a published, committed artifact.** It carries per-stage completion, per-doc `authored_hash`/`rendered_hash`/`rendered_from`, outstanding work-order coordination (`outstanding`, `author_batches`), generator provenance (`generators`), and capability probes -- and it is committed with all seven orgs. `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` argues these want opposite semantics (execution wants mutation, provenance wants immutability and audit) and proposes a three-way split: run state, build manifest, artifact index.
- **Why deferred:** the tension is real in principle and has not bitten in practice, and the fix costs the one property the file exists to provide. CLAUDE.md's hard rule is "Resume state is file-derived, never conversation memory", and `state.json` is the single source of resume truth; splitting it creates three files that can disagree with each other, which is the failure mode `SPEC.md` (M11b) explicitly refused when it rejected a fleet-level progress ledger: "a new ledger would be state that can disagree with the files". The file is also deliberately timestamp-free so regeneration stays byte-stable, which is most of what the "immutability" half of the critique wants. Splitting it would also move or break `orgsmith/state@1` for every committed org.
- **The critique's stated trigger is worth recording because it is testable:** it expects this to matter "when M12 runs are large, distributed, resumed across machines, or generated with multiple workers". Three of those four already happened at M11b -- 38 batches, windows 9 wide, a cross-session resume after an ~80-minute idle with 9 orders outstanding, all nine re-dispatched and ingested with zero duplicated or lost documents (`docs/SCALE.md`). Distributed across machines is the one that has not, and nothing plans it.
- **Revisit criteria:** generation is ever distributed across machines or filesystems (the case the current design genuinely does not cover); or a resume bug is traced to the file carrying two kinds of state; or `state.json` needs a schema bump for another reason, since the split is cheapest to do at the same time; or an external consumer needs build provenance without the run state attached to it.
- **Origin:** `docs/EXTERNAL-CRITIQUE-2026-07-17.md` ("state.json mixes execution state and published provenance").


### recipe-coherence-test-has-no-floor
- **`test_fleet_recipe_growth_headcount_and_span_describe_one_firm` is one-sided: it asserts `margins[-1] < _NET_MARGIN_CEILING` (0.40) and has no lower bound** (`tests/test_org_regen.py:112,252`). It catches the incoherence it was built for -- a firm compounding fees against a frozen roster, posting a margin no professional-services firm posts -- and nothing else. A recipe whose `base_revenue` is far too low for its headcount posts a deeply negative margin and passes, because a negative number is less than 0.40.
- **Found 2026-07-17 by trying to publish the opposite.** A README sentence claimed the coherence test would reject the absurd-low-revenue tuning that would close the fee/revenue gap. It would not. The claim was checked before the commit landed and the README now says the true thing, including that the test misses this.
- **Why deferred:** no recipe is anywhere near the floor, so this is a gap in what the test *could* catch rather than a bug it is missing today (measured 2026-07-17: every fleet org posts a healthy positive margin, and the entry that motivated the test, `dev-mini-margin-incoherent`, is a *ceiling* case at 43.1%). Adding a floor means choosing a number, and unlike the ceiling -- where "no professional-services firm posts a 50% net margin" is a defensible anchor -- the floor has no obvious one: real firms do post losses, especially young ones, and the founding year is already excluded as a deliberate 0.45x ramp. A badly-chosen floor would fail an honest recipe.
- **Revisit criteria:** any recipe is written whose intended margin is negative or near zero (the floor stops being hypothetical and the number can be chosen against a real case); or the fee/revenue coupling from `engagement-ledger-reads-as-whole-book` lands, since deriving `base_revenue` from the engagement book makes low-revenue recipes reachable by accident rather than by typo; or the test is touched for any other reason.
- **Origin:** spec 2026-07-17 (found while verifying a README claim about the test, which was false).
