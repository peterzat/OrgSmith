Brackenridge Civil Group is a small civil engineering practice in a
county seat, founded in 1996 and working for municipal public works
departments, a regional water authority, and two private developers. Its
work is site grading, stormwater, roadway, and utility design: drawings
go out under a sealed cover sheet, and the paperwork that surrounds them
is typed in Word and printed for the job file.

It is a firm of its era, and the file share shows it. Proposals and
design memoranda are Word documents saved to a lettered project folder.
Executed agreements come back countersigned by fax, get scanned on the
flatbed by the office administrator, and land in the share as crooked,
speckled images. Progress meetings with the county produce minutes that
list every attendee by full name and agency. The annual numbers live in
one spreadsheet the Principal Engineer keeps himself.

Writing is late-1990s American professional-services prose: complete
sentences, courteous but not warm, no buzzwords that would postdate the
document. Engineers refer to clients by agency name and to each other by
full name in anything that leaves the office. The Principal Engineer
signs every agreement and every letter to a public body. Nobody writes
"reach out"; they write "we will contact".
